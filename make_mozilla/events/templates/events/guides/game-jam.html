How to host

Game Jam

Create a HTML5 based game! Team up to transform your ideas into real world games.

Create an event

Overview

A Game Jam aims to:

  • Provide access to innovative new gaming technologies that allow you to create the ultimate browser based game.
  • Bring together people who love making and hacking games as much as you do.
  • Inspire you to dream up a cool new game and help you build a prototype that demonstrates your idea.
  • Prepare you to enter your best work possible into the Game On competition.

Sound like fun? This guide will help! It's a community document, so please share your ideas to help improve it.

Facts and figures
  • 10 - 50 participants
  • 24 - 48 hours
  • At a school, office, computer lab or any open hacking space

Before

Engage your participants beforehand
  • Have conversations with participants before the event:
    • What kinds of games are they interested in making and doing?
    • What do they need to make that happen?
    • How can your event serve them?
  • Know who you're doing this for. Participants at successful game jams know what they are making and for whom. You can use the Game On contest as a great end goal!
  • Think about what skills you need in the room. Invite people from other areas who can help in valuable ways. (Design, CS, ECE, etc)
Craft a good design challenge:
  • A design challenge is a question that you set out to address together.
  • Write in simple, clear language and include the target users in what you're building.
  • For example:
    • What games can we make that teach youth an important web skill?
    • What game mechanics can we design for mobile interfaces?
  • These are just ideas to get started. You can always keep it open-ended or go with a general gaming theme or category (i.e. mobile, hackable, learning) If you'd like help crafting a game jam challenge, please get in touch with us!
Find a space and a date
  • Find a comfortable, flexible space. Ideal is an open space where you can move the chairs and sit together in a circle.
  • Think about wifi and power, good lighting, and snacks.
  • Pick a date. Give yourself enough time and make sure the date works for your participants.
Spread the word
  • Read our guidelines on planning safe events.
  • Write a short description and create a page for your event. (Facebook event pages are great)
  • Invite key participants. Identify important skillsets for the event. Talk to local communities, forums, universities to find the right participants. You can also contact the Mozilla team for advice.

During

Event Logistics
  • Have the group sit in a circle or clusters near each other. This creates a sense of cooperation and helps everyone see each other.
  • Do a quick go-round. Introduce yourself and the event and then ask the participants to introduce themselves.
  • Give a brief overview. What will you make today?
  • Discuss the concept of a "minimum viable prototype." You're aiming to produce something that works, even if it's very rough around the edges, by the end of the event. Prototypes include:
    • A napkin sketch
    • A blog post
    • A little bit of code
  • Break into small groups
  • In small teams, start brainstorming about the challenge and write down as many solution as possible. Rough notes and sketches are fine!
  • Regroup and present the best ideas, collecting feedback.
  • Create a shared repository such as an etherpad, a wiki, or a wall of paper, to collect all the ideas.
Start Building
  • Go back into teams and focus on the ideas that got the most traction.
  • Start drafting the prototypes on paper. This helps formulate ideas and exposes any holes.
  • Consider design elements to simplify and improve the user's experience.
Playtesting Round
  • Periodically playtest each others projects. You can do quick play rounds with the whole group or invite people to walk around and share what they're working on.
  • Identify skill-sets and needs, such as "Group 1 needs a developer" or "Group 2 needs a designer," so participants can help each other where needed.
  • If a group gets stuck, encourage them to keep going to the best of their ability or rethink the idea to fit what they can build.
Sprint to Finish
  • Keep refining your prototype or using other techniques and tools to get closer to a workable solution.
  • Teams sprint to finish their "minimum viable prototype."
  • Each team prepares a short demo or presentation of their project.
  • Celebrate what you made. Back in a large circle, have participants present their prototypes.
  • Encourage participants to think about what they want to do next with their project. Hopefully this will be the beginning of their submission for Game On!
  • If you like, please share your prototypes with the world!. You can publish the links and other materials to a blog or the social network of your choice.
  • Please tag your work with #mozgames.

After

Wrap Up
  • Ask everyone how it went. Encourage feedback about the event and the projects. What did they learn? What did they like? If they have suggestions for making it better, be sure to take notes.
  • Talk about continuing to hack together. You may want to keep building these prototypes or try new ones. Talk about what you would like to do next, such as organize another game jam.
Set a date.

If there's interest, pick a date for the next time!

Let us know how it went
  • Please share your experience about hosting the event and the prototypes, including links to the things you made.
  • We'll send an email reminder after the event to collect your feedback.
Support

If you need help at any time before, during or after your event, please contact us. We have a team of volunteers and staff members dedicated to helping organizers like you.
