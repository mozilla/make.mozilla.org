Overview

A Hack Jam aims to:

  • Team up different skill-sets to explore an interesting challenge.
  • Collaboratively build a "minimum viable prototype" to demonstrate your idea.

Sound like fun? This guide will help!

It's a community document, so please share your thoughts.

Before

Decide what you want to make
  • Talk with participants about their interests.
  • Decide on a challenge or question to explore together. For example:
    • How can we add value to a story using live web content?
    • What games can we make that teach an important skill?
    • How can we harness sensor signals to navigate a space?
  • These are just ideas to get started. If you'd like help crafting a hack jam challenge, please get in touch or see what others have done.
Find a space and a date
  • Find a comfortable, flexible space. Ideal is an open space where you can move the chairs and sit together in a circle.
  • Think about wifi and power, good lighting, and snacks.
  • Pick a date. Give yourself enough time and make sure the date works for your friends.
Spread the word
  • Write a short description about your event.
  • Post it on make.mozilla.org/events or the organizing platform of your choice.
  • Invite key participants. Identify important skillsets for the event. Talk to local communities to find the right participants. You can also contact the Mozilla team for advice.

During

Introduce your event
  • Have the group sit in a circle. This creates a sense of cooperation and helps everyone see each other.
  • Do a quick go-round. Ask the participants to introduce themselves.
  • Give a brief overview. What will you make today?
Break into small groups
  • In small teams, start brainstorming about the challenge and write down as many ideas as possible.
  • Napkin-sketch the best ideas.
  • Regroup and present the sketches, collecting feedback.
Start Hacking
  • Back in teams, paper prototype your idea.
  • Draft each step on paper. This gives clarity to your idea and exposes any holes in the process.
  • If possible, try coding your prototype. Else, keep refining the paper version.
  • Consider design elements to simplify and improve the user's experience.
Iterative Feedback
  • Periodically give feedback on the projects. You can do quick share-back rounds with the group.
  • Identify skill-sets and needs, such as "Group 1 needs a developer" or "Group 2 needs a designer," so participants can help each other where needed.
  • If a group gets stuck, encourage them to keep going to the best of their ability or rethink the idea to fit what they can build.
Sprint to Finish
  • Teams sprint to finish their "minimum viable prototype."
  • Create a shared repository such as an etherpad, a wiki, or a wall of paper, to collect all the prototypes.
  • Each team prepares a short demo or presentation of their project.
Share your hacks
  • Share what you made back in the circle.
  • Encourage participants to think about what they want to do next with their project.
  • If you like, let the world know what you made!
    • Paste the URL in this etherpad
    • or tweet the hack and tag it #mozparty
    • or upload a picture to a gallery, such as Flickr, and tag it with #mozparty.

After

Wrap Up
  • Ask the group about the event. What did they like? What could be improved?
  • Take notes on the feedback. And be sure you have a copy of each project.
  • Decide on next steps. If there's interest, pick a date for the next time!
Let us know how it went
  • Please share your experience about hosting the event and the projects, including links to the things you made.
  • We’ll send an email reminder after the event to collect your feedback.
  • Thanks for running a great event, and keep hacking!
